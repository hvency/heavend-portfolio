Chapter 01

The Beginning

The Aswang

Myth or Misunderstood Legend?

Some stories are meant to entertain. Others survive because generations of people believed they were worth remembering.

Across the Philippine archipelago, long before horror films, documentaries, or social media, stories traveled by word of mouth. They were shared on bamboo porches after sunset, told during long walks home from the fields, repeated while children gathered around a kerosene lamp, and whispered whenever the night seemed a little too quiet.

Many of those stories have faded with time.

One never did.

It begins with a single word.

Aswang.

For centuries, that name has inspired fear, curiosity, and endless debate. It appears in old Spanish accounts, anthropological studies, local folklore, paintings, literature, films, television programs, and everyday conversations. Few figures in Philippine folklore have been told, retold, and reimagined as often as the Aswang, yet few are as difficult to define.

Ask ten Filipinos what an Aswang is and you may hear ten different answers.

In Capiz, someone may describe a quiet neighbor who appears ordinary during the day but secretly hunts at night. In parts of the Visayas, another might speak of a creature that slips through the darkness in search of unborn children. Elsewhere, it becomes a vampire, a witch, a corpse-stealing ghoul, a shape-shifting beast, or a mysterious figure blamed for illnesses no one could explain.

The details change from one province to another. The names change. The appearance changes. Yet the story remains unmistakably familiar.

This diversity puzzled scholars who attempted to study Philippine folklore as a whole. How could one legendary creature possess so many forms?

Filipino folklorist Maximo D. Ramos offered an explanation that continues to shape modern understanding of the legend. The word Aswang functions as an umbrella term for several supernatural beings.

These include vampire-like beings, witches, shape-shifting creatures, corpse-eating ghouls, and viscera suckers preserved through oral traditions across different communities.

Instead of asking “What does the Aswang look like?” scholars began asking: Which community is telling the story?

The Philippines is home to hundreds of languages and traditions. Before written records, stories evolved differently across islands and generations.

The Aswang became less a single monster and more a collection of living traditions.

Its story changed because the people telling it changed.

Chapter 02

The Name Behind the Legend

Where Did the Word "Aswang" Come From?

Every legend has a beginning.

Some begin with a place. Others begin with a person.

The story of the Aswang begins with a single word—one that has been spoken across the Philippine archipelago for centuries, yet whose true origins remain surprisingly difficult to trace.

Unlike many mythical creatures whose names can be linked to a single language or historical record, the word "Aswang" has no universally accepted origin.

Historians, folklorists, and linguists have proposed several theories over the years, but none has been proven conclusively.

By the time European chroniclers arrived in the sixteenth century, the word was already familiar to many communities.

The Aswang was not created by the Spanish.

It was already part of the beliefs of the people they encountered.

Other Spanish writers, including Pedro Chirino and Tomás Ortiz, also described creatures linked to the Aswang.

However, these documents should be read with context.

The missionaries were not preserving folklore — they were interpreting it through a colonial lens.

As a result, early records reflect both indigenous belief systems and European religious perspectives.

The question of where the word "Aswang" originated is equally complex.

Some researchers suggest Visayan linguistic roots, while others point to Austronesian connections.

No single explanation has been universally accepted.

Rather than a gap in history, scholars see this as evidence of how folklore evolves.

Stories move with people. Languages change. Meanings shift.

The Aswang is no exception.

Its name has survived far longer than its original explanation.

Every community gave the Aswang its own face.

It appears in manuscripts, folklore, films, documentaries, and everyday conversation.

But its story did not begin with ink on paper.

It began with people.

Chapter 03

Before the Legend Was Written

The Earliest Records of the Aswang

Every legend has a beginning.

The Aswang is no exception.

Long before it appeared in films, novels, television documentaries, or horror stories shared online, the Aswang already lived in the beliefs of communities scattered across the Philippine archipelago. It was not invented by a single storyteller, nor did it emerge from one province or one generation. By the time outsiders first attempted to describe it, the legend had already existed for centuries in countless oral traditions.

That is one of the greatest challenges in tracing the history of the Aswang.

Unlike kingdoms that leave behind monuments or battles preserved in official records, folklore rarely begins on paper. It survives through memory. Parents teach it to their children. Elders pass it to younger generations. Entire communities preserve it through storytelling, ritual, and everyday conversation until, eventually, someone writes it down.

For the Aswang, that moment arrived during the Spanish colonization of the Philippines.

When Spanish missionaries and chroniclers reached the islands in the sixteenth century, they encountered beliefs unlike anything they had known in Europe. Alongside accounts of local customs, religious practices, and village life, they recorded stories about mysterious beings feared by the people they sought to convert.

Among the earliest references appears in the writings of Juan de Plasencia, whose Customs of the Tagalogs (1589) documented various indigenous beliefs and supernatural beings. Although he did not describe a single creature matching every modern image of the Aswang, his work mentioned figures that would later become associated with the broader Aswang tradition.

Other missionaries followed.

Pedro Chirino described indigenous spiritual beliefs during the early seventeenth century. More than a century later, Tomás Ortiz included accounts of supernatural creatures in Practica del Ministerio, while Joaquín Martínez de Zúñiga documented stories that continued to circulate during the late Spanish colonial period.

Taken together, these writings became the earliest surviving historical records of what would eventually be known as the Aswang.

But these documents must be read with care.

The Spanish friars were not folklorists.

They were missionaries whose primary goal was to spread Christianity throughout the islands. Many indigenous beliefs were interpreted through a Christian worldview, where unfamiliar spirits, rituals, and local religious leaders were often described as superstition, witchcraft, or the work of the devil.

To the missionaries, many of these stories were evidence of pagan practices that needed to be replaced.

To the communities who told them, however, they formed part of a much older understanding of the world.

This distinction is important.

Modern historians caution that colonial records reveal as much about the perspectives of the writers as they do about the beliefs of the people they described. Rather than treating these accounts as objective truth, scholars compare them with oral traditions, local folklore, and later ethnographic studies to build a more complete picture of the legend.

Research over the last century has shown that belief in Aswang-like beings likely predates Spanish arrival.

Anthropologists and historians argue that many stories grew from indigenous animistic traditions in which the natural and supernatural worlds existed side by side.

Forests, rivers, mountains, and even ordinary villages were believed to be inhabited by unseen beings capable of helping—or harming—those who crossed their path.

As different ethnolinguistic groups developed across the islands, so did their own versions of these supernatural creatures.

Though each tradition evolved independently, many would eventually be grouped under the single name Aswang.

Colonization also changed the legend itself.

As Christianity spread across the Philippines, older beliefs did not simply disappear. Instead, many blended with new religious ideas.

Some scholars—including Carolyn Brewer, Raul Pertierra, and Lumina Alinea Aquino—suggest that colonial narratives gradually associated indigenous spiritual leaders, particularly the babaylan, with witchcraft and demonic imagery.

Powerful women who once served as healers, ritual specialists, and community leaders were increasingly portrayed by colonial writers as servants of evil or connected to malevolent forces.

Over time, the boundary between feared supernatural creatures and feared members of society became less distinct.

The legend continued to evolve.

By the nineteenth century, stories about the Aswang had become deeply woven into everyday life across many parts of the Philippines.

Travelers recorded local encounters. Villagers warned one another about suspicious strangers. Parents repeated old stories to their children.

Even as towns expanded and new beliefs emerged, the legend adapted rather than disappeared.

The Aswang survived because it was never confined to a single book.

It lived wherever people continued telling the story.

And with every generation, the legend gained another chapter.

Chapter 04

A World Where Spirits Walked Beside People

Understanding the Beliefs Behind the Legend

It is easy to dismiss the Aswang as nothing more than an old ghost story.

It is much harder to understand why generations of people believed in it.

To do that, we have to leave behind the modern world for a moment.

Imagine a time before electricity illuminated villages after sunset. Before hospitals could explain disease. Before police investigated unexplained deaths. Before weather forecasts, forensic science, or modern medicine could provide answers to life's greatest uncertainties.

For many early Filipino communities, the world was alive with unseen forces.

The forest was not simply a forest.

A river was not merely flowing water.

A mountain was more than stone and earth.

Everything possessed a presence.

Some spirits protected.

Others punished.

Some simply existed alongside humanity, asking only to be respected.

This understanding of the world—commonly described by scholars as animism—formed the foundation of many indigenous belief systems long before the arrival of Christianity.

Nature, ancestors, spirits, and human life were deeply interconnected. The visible and invisible worlds were not separate realms but parts of the same reality.

Within that world, stories of beings like the Aswang did not seem impossible.

They seemed possible.

Many communities believed that illness, misfortune, failed harvests, mysterious disappearances, or sudden deaths could have causes beyond the physical world.

Not every tragedy had an obvious explanation.

A healthy child could suddenly become sick.

A pregnant woman might lose her unborn child without warning.

Someone who appeared strong one day might be dead the next.

Without the medical knowledge available today, these events were often understood through the spiritual beliefs of the community.

The Aswang became one possible explanation for the unexplainable.

Rather than representing chaos alone, the legend gave frightening events a recognizable face.

Fear became a story.

And stories are often easier to carry than uncertainty.

These beliefs were also shaped by the people who served as spiritual leaders within their communities.

Long before Spanish missionaries introduced Catholicism, many villages looked to the babaylan for guidance.

They acted as healers, ritual specialists, spiritual mediators, and keepers of traditional knowledge.

During ceremonies, they were believed to communicate with ancestral spirits and unseen beings, seeking protection, healing, or guidance for the community.

Their role extended far beyond religion.

They were respected advisers, caretakers of cultural traditions, and, in many communities, influential leaders.

Because of this close connection to the spiritual world, the babaylan occupied a unique place in society.

They understood the unseen forces that many believed shaped everyday life, including those associated with harmful spirits.

When Spanish missionaries arrived, however, this worldview was challenged.

Colonial writings frequently portrayed indigenous religious practices as superstition, while some babaylan were described as practitioners of witchcraft or servants of demonic forces.

Historians such as Carolyn Brewer, Lumina Alinea Aquino, and Alfred McCoy argue that this shift reflected broader efforts to replace indigenous religious authority with Christianity.

As beliefs changed, so too did the stories.

The line between respected spiritual leaders and feared supernatural beings became increasingly blurred in colonial accounts.

Belief in the Aswang was never only about monsters.

It was also about community.

Across many parts of the Philippines, stories about the Aswang encouraged people to follow customs designed to protect both themselves and those around them.

Children were warned not to wander after dark.

Travelers avoided isolated paths at night.

Families remained close to women who had recently given birth.

Neighbors watched over one another during wakes and funerals.

Whether these practices protected people from supernatural creatures or from the very real dangers of darkness, wild animals, illness, or crime, the result was often the same.

The stories reinforced caution.

In this way, folklore became more than entertainment.

It became part of everyday life.

The Aswang also reflected the fears of each community.

In farming villages, dense forests concealed unfamiliar sounds and unseen animals.

Along rivers and coastlines, darkness hid dangerous currents and predators.

Far from neighboring settlements, silence itself could become unsettling.

When fear has no visible source, imagination often provides one.

The Aswang gave shape to that uncertainty.

It became the shadow at the edge of the field.

The figure glimpsed beneath the trees.

The strange sound carried by the wind after midnight.

Modern scholars continue to study the legend not because they seek proof that the Aswang exists, but because the stories reveal something equally important.

They reveal how people understand fear.

Anthropologists such as Frank Lynch and Raul Pertierra have shown that folklore often reflects the values, anxieties, and social relationships of the communities that preserve it.

The Aswang became part of a shared cultural language through which people explained danger, protected one another, and passed knowledge across generations.

Whether or not anyone believed every story literally, the legend endured because it fulfilled a purpose.

It offered explanations where there were none.

It strengthened traditions.

It connected families through storytelling.

And it reminded every generation that the unknown deserves respect.

Today, science explains many mysteries that once frightened entire villages.

Yet the stories remain.

Not because they defeated science—

but because they became something greater than belief.

They became part of the Filipino imagination.

And perhaps that is why, centuries later, the Aswang still walks beside us—not in the shadows of the forest, but in the stories we continue to tell.

Chapter 05

The Many Faces of the Aswang

One Name. Countless Nightmares.

If you asked ten Filipinos to describe an Aswang, you might receive ten different answers.

One would tell you about a beautiful woman who secretly drinks human blood.

Another would swear it is a winged creature that separates its upper body from its legs before flying into the night.

Someone else would insist it transforms into a dog, a pig, or even a large black bird.

A fisherman from another province might speak of corpse-stealing creatures that lurk near cemeteries, while an elder from a distant village may warn you about witches capable of making people ill without ever touching them.

Each description sounds like a different monster.

Yet somehow, every storyteller calls it the same thing.

The Aswang.

For generations, this puzzled historians, anthropologists, and folklorists.

How could one creature possess so many forms?

The answer, according to Filipino folklorist Maximo D. Ramos, is that it never did.

Drawing from years of fieldwork across the Philippines, Ramos concluded that the Aswang is best understood not as a single mythical creature but as a collective name for several distinct supernatural beings preserved in Philippine folklore.

While every region developed its own stories, many eventually became grouped beneath one familiar name.

His works—particularly The Aswang Syncrasy in Philippine Folklore and Creatures of Philippine Lower Mythology—remain among the most influential studies of these legends.

Ramos identified five major categories commonly associated with the Aswang.

Each possesses its own appearance.

Its own habits.

Its own victims.

Its own place within Philippine folklore.

Some stalk the living.

Others prey upon the dead.

Some fly through the night.

Others hide in plain sight among ordinary people.

Although these creatures are often grouped together under the name Aswang, each has its own history, regional traditions, and distinctive characteristics.

Understanding these differences helps explain why the legend has endured for centuries and why no two Filipinos tell exactly the same story.

The next five scrolls explore each of these legendary beings individually, revealing how every one reflects a different fear, belief, and tradition within Filipino culture.

Chapter 06

The Vampire

The Silent Predator Hidden in Plain Sight

📜 Creature Record

Classification Aswang
Type Vampire
Activity Nocturnal
Primary Victims Sleeping Humans
Known Regions Bicol • Cebu • Visayas • Ilocos
Distinctive Trait Long needle-like tongue used to feed on blood
Common Appearance An ordinary man or woman during the day
Folklore Role One of the five major Aswang classifications identified by Maximo D. Ramos

Unlike the pale, cape-wearing vampires of European folklore, the Filipino vampire often appears completely ordinary.

By day, it may live among its neighbors unnoticed—a beautiful young woman, a quiet spouse, or a familiar face within the community.

Nothing about its appearance immediately reveals its true nature.

Night is when it hunts.

Traditional accounts describe the creature feeding by extending an unnaturally long, needle-like tongue.

Using its pointed tip, it pierces the skin and quietly drinks blood while its victim sleeps.

Rather than attacking openly, it is said to feed gradually over many nights, leaving the victim weaker with every visit until illness—or even death—follows.

Because these attacks were believed to happen silently and without obvious wounds, unexplained weakness or wasting illnesses were sometimes blamed on this form of the Aswang.

Regional traditions vary considerably.

Stories describing vampire-like Aswang have been recorded in parts of Bicol, Cebu, the Visayas, and among some Ilocano communities.

Although the details differ from one province to another, the central idea remains remarkably consistent: an ordinary-looking individual hides a terrifying secret that emerges only after darkness falls.

This ability to blend seamlessly into everyday life is perhaps what makes the vampire one of the most unsettling forms of the Aswang.

Its greatest weapon is not monstrous appearance—

but invisibility.

Chapter 07

The Viscera Sucker

The Creature That Gave Rise to the Manananggal

📜 Creature Record

Classification Aswang
Type Viscera Sucker
Activity Nocturnal
Primary Victims Pregnant Women and Unborn Children
Known Regions Bicol • Luzon • Western Visayas
Distinctive Trait Separates its upper body from its lower half before flying
Common Appearance Ordinary human during the day
Also Known As Manananggal

Few creatures in Philippine folklore are as visually striking as the viscera sucker.

By daylight, it appears entirely human.

By night, however, the creature is said to separate its upper body from its lower half before taking flight.

Its enormous bat-like wings carry it silently across rooftops while long strands of entrails hang beneath its floating body.

Few images in Philippine folklore are as unforgettable—or as unsettling.

Its preferred weapon is not its claws—

but its tongue.

Traditional accounts describe it as long, hollow, and tube-like, capable of reaching through windows, bamboo walls, or even small openings in a roof to feed upon sleeping victims.

Pregnant women and unborn children are among those most commonly mentioned in traditional stories, making this one of the most feared forms of the Aswang.

Today, many Filipinos know this creature by another name:

The Manananggal.

Although often treated as its own legendary being, folklorists such as Maximo D. Ramos classify it as one manifestation within the broader Aswang tradition.

Stories of viscera suckers are especially widespread in Bicol, parts of Luzon, and Western Visayas, although every region preserves its own unique version of the legend.

Some traditions describe the creature as hunting alone, while others portray it as living secretly among ordinary people until nightfall reveals its true nature.

Across all versions, however, one image remains remarkably consistent:

A figure rising into the darkness, leaving half of its body behind.

Chapter 08

The Were-Beast

When the Hunter Becomes the Beast

📜 Creature Record

Classification Aswang
Type Were-Beast
Activity Nocturnal
Primary Victims Travelers, Livestock, and Isolated People
Known Regions Bicol • Cebu • Western Visayas • Luzon
Distinctive Trait Transforms into a savage animal after sunset
Common Appearance Ordinary man or woman during the day
Common Animal Forms Dog • Wild Boar • Cat • Other Local Beasts

Not every Aswang flies.

Some run.

This form of the Aswang is believed to live as an ordinary man or woman during the day before transforming into a savage animal after sunset.

Unlike the European werewolf, Philippine folklore reflects the animals familiar to local communities.

Depending on the region, the creature may become a dog, wild boar, cat, or another fearsome beast.

For this reason, scholars often prefer the broader term were-beast rather than werewolf.

Traditional accounts describe these creatures roaming villages after midnight, attacking livestock, frightening travelers, or preying upon isolated victims.

Unlike other forms of the Aswang that rely on stealth or supernatural abilities, the were-beast embodies raw physical strength and ferocity.

Because the transformation was believed to occur in secret, suspicion could easily fall upon neighbors whose behavior seemed unusual or who frequently disappeared after dark.

Such stories sometimes reflected not only fear of monsters but also fear of the unknown within one's own community.

Legends of were-beast Aswang have been documented across Bicol, Cebu, Western Visayas, and parts of Luzon.

While the specific animal changes from one region to another, the central idea remains remarkably consistent: an ordinary human conceals a terrifying second nature that emerges only after nightfall.

It is this uncertainty—the inability to know who might secretly become the beast—that has allowed the legend to survive for generations.
